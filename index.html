Academia

Teaching

A lot of my time at school is spent teaching, and I wouldn't have it any other way. I've found it to be very fulfilling, and it's also helped me learn the material on a much deeper level. My teaching experiences have been an invaluable part of my education. I'm involved in teaching at Berkeley in two main capacities:
  1. Head TA for EE 120, UC Berkeley's Signals and Systems class. EE 120 is typically the first upper division electrical engineering class students take. It covers linear system theory, Fourier analysis, and some sampling and control theory. I've served as an EE 120 TA for two semesters now (Fall 2018, Spring 2019).
    One of the best things about being a TA is how much impact I'm able to have on improving the class. This semester, as head TA, I'm leading several course development efforts:
    • iPython Labs. EE 120 is a fairly theoretical class aimed at giving students a lot of the mathematical foundations needed for electrical engineering. Applications of the content have traditionally been left to future classes. As a result, the material can often seem disconnected from the real world. To help bridge this gap, I'm working, along with a fantastic team of TAs, on developing iPython notebook labs to supplement the material. The labs cover Scientific Computing in Python (numpy, matplotlib, iPython notebook), Convolution and Filtering, The Fast Fourier Transform, 1D Signal and 2D Image Deconvolution, Orthogonal Signaling, and PID Control. I'm very excited to add this hands-on component to the class this semester, and grateful for my fellow staff members' help in making this happen.
    • Discussion Section Worksheets. Berkeley courses typically consist of lectures from the professor, followed by TA-led discussion sections to do practice problems on the content. In Fall 2018, we created a brand new set of worksheets + solutions for these discussion sections for the students. Before we created these, none existed for the class. This semester, we're iterating on that first set of worksheets to give students an even better resource.
  2. EE 16A Coordinator for Computer Science Mentors (CSM). CSM is a club at UC Berkeley that offers auxiliary tutoring sections of 4-6 students for all of Berkeley's lower division EECS courses. This allows students to have a more personalized learning experience in classes that have enrollments of 1000+ students each semester, and has helped the department's classes scale with the massive surge in demand of the past few years. I've been involved in CSM for three semesters now, all with EE 16A, the introductory electrical engineering class for freshmen. As a mentor, I have taught EE 16A sections on topics including linear algebra, circuit theory, and optimization. As EE 16A Coordinator, I have the additional responsibilities of interviewing and selecting new mentors from our applicant pool and overseeing all of CSM's EE 16A operations (printing worksheets, booking rooms for sections, managing all 30+ mentors), all of which are shared responsibilities with a Co-Coordinator.
A copy of my teaching reviews (as both a TA and a CS Mentor) are available upon request.
